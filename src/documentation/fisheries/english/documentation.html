Introduction

Decisions regarding conservation problems are complex because they deal with interactions among people, their livelihoods, and natural resources. Moreover, decisions may involve controversial or poorly understood issues such as the status of species, equity, or property rights. Selecting a fishery that has the right set of characteristics to be successful in the Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) certification program is one such conservation challenge.

Delphos: Fisheries is a decision-making tool for selecting suitable candidate fisheries for MSC certification. It can be used to evaluate fisheries anywhere in the world and in any size region. The program will guide you through a data-gathering and analysis process that is compatible with your institutional and project goals.

Background

Through the application of a global environmental standard for sustainable and well-managed fisheries and its blue ecolabel, the MSC seeks to harness consumer purchasing power to promote environmentally responsible stewardship of the world's fisheries. MSC certification not only provides long-term security for fishing communities, but also offers fisheries and stakeholders an opportunity to improve management practices and advocate for better data collection, new laws, and stronger enforcement measures.

Each fishery that applies for MSC certification is evaluated by an independent assessment team with regard to a standard set of principles and criteria concerning the 1) health of the target stock, 2) effects of the fishery on the ecosystem, and 3) quality of the management system. For details about the MSC standard, please see http://www.msc.org.

Early critics of the MSC contended that the certification process was geared toward large-scale industrial fisheries, and inaccessible to small-scale community fisheries due to extensive data requirements and prohibitive costs. However, many small-scale fisheries are healthy and well-managed. Small-scale fisheries are also of enormous global importance, as small-scale fishers comprise approximately 94% of the world's fishers and produce nearly half of the global fish supply for human consumption. Small-scale fisheries provide most of the protein and jobs for coastal communities around the world and operate in some of the most biologically rich and diverse places on earth.

WWF's Community Fisheries Program helps small-scale fisheries through the MSC certification process. WWF has been working on small-scale fisheries certification since 1999, emphasizing the participation of local fishers and the importance of traditional knowledge in the certification process, and thereby engaging communities in protecting the resources they rely upon.

Program History

In the spring of 2000, Comunidad y Biodiversidad A.C. (COBI), in partnership with the WWF Community Fisheries Program, wondered how to select the best candidate for MSC certification from dozens of community fisheries on the Baja California peninsula. Because we needed to consider different institutional objectives from various project partners, we designed a “pre-analysis” method to systematize our decision-making process. By convening an expert panel to gather data and using a multicriteria analysis process, we selected the Baja California red spiny lobster, Panulirus interruptus, as our preferred MSC candidate fishery. In April 2004, this fishery became the first small-scale fishery from a developing country to receive MSC certification.

This versatile software program is based on the pre-analysis process that COBI and the WWF Community Fisheries Program developed to select a suitable candidate fishery for MSC certification.

Multicriteria Analysis/Evamix

Multicriteria analysis is a versatile tool for evaluating environmental and conservation questions. We selected multicriteria analysis as the appropriate decision-making tool to select candidates for MSC certification because it can handle both qualitative and quantitative criteria and allows a question to be evaluated from different points of view.

Multicriteria analysis allows the inclusion of both qualitative and quantitative information because it does not require converting all data involved in the assessment to the same unit. This flexibility allows one to include very vague criteria, such as the "community's current interest in MSC certification,” as well as hard data, such as "catch volume.”

Institutional or program goals may be expressed by weighting – or giving importance to – certain criteria. The data can then be analyzed multiple times from the perspectives of various project partners. For example, one partner may wish to work with a fishery in a community that is actively involved in the management system, while another partner finds it more important that the fishery has strong national and international markets. Using multicriteria analysis, partners may assign importance to each criterion as determined by their individual goals, and the fisheries can be evaluated from each point of view. The results from each analysis provide the basis for discussion among project partners about which fishery to select for the MSC certification process.

The multicriteria approach used in Delphos is the Evamix method, a matrix-based evaluation and ranking tool. The Evamix algorithm results in a relative ranking of each fishery that incorporates all of the criteria and criteria weights. Evamix is an ideal tool for our purposes of analyzing and comparing fisheries because it is simple and flexible, and allows the user to clearly define both qualitative and quantitative evaluation criteria.

The Delphos Process

The following pages will step you through the pre-analysis process: project planning, information gathering, and multicriteria analysis. Each link in the chain depends, to an extent, on the one before. As such, it is important to think through the entire process before jumping into a project. Brainstorming the outcome of each step in advance will help ensure that you gain the most from each step.

The pre-analysis is meant to give you a better understanding of the fisheries that exist in a given geographical area and which might be the best candidate for MSC certification. This process is not meant to expose larger political or cultural issues related to fisheries. While you can learn about some of these issues during discussions with local experts, it is essential to be aware of the political realities of your region of interest before beginning the pre-analysis, and to account for them in your project goals and design.

1. Define Your Goals

There are a number of reasons why an organization might want to explore MSC certification for fisheries in a given region. Alternatively, a group might want to use the pre-analysis process not with MSC certification in mind, but simply to create a database of information.

Your reasons for using this program will impact the way you approach the pre-analysis process, from how you define the geographic region to the weights you apply to criteria. Having a clear understanding from the beginning of what you seek to accomplish will make the entire process flow more smoothly and produce more meaningful and applicable results.

2. Define Your Timeline

It is important to consider the timeline for your project during your initial brainstorming and project design. Think about how much time you intend to spend on each step of the pre-analysis process to achieve the goals you have defined. Establishing a timeline will help you avoid taking on too much.

For example, if you want to see pre-analysis results within one year, it would be unwise to look at a very large region that includes a lot of fisheries, such as multiple states or countries. On the other hand, if you are interested in a large region, it is important to realize that your timeline may be closer to two years.

In addition to considering the time needed to complete the pre-analysis, you might also want to consider the time needed to complete goals beyond this point, such as MSC assessment of the selected fishery. Depending on the size and complexity of the fishery, the actual certification process can become quite lengthy. Thinking through this step will help refine expectations.

3. Define the Region

This model can be used anywhere in the world and in any size region. However, selecting and defining your region of interest is a critical part of both the pre-analysis process and MSC certification. It is important to keep in mind that the certification process can often be long and challenging. While conducting a pre-analysis can help avoid some of the obstacles associated with this kind of work, trying to tackle too large a region will create additional difficulties. It is critical that the realities of your financial and human resources agree with the realities of the region that you choose to work in.

If you are considering a very large region, it is beneficial to compartmentalize the project. You could do this based on management components, as may be the case in a multi-state country, or by biological components, such as habitats. By dividing the region in this way, you will essentially be dealing with a number of mini-projects as opposed to one very large project. Alternatively, consider partnering with other organizations throughout the region that can locally execute the following chain of steps.

In addition to considering the size of the region, it is important to consider your overall goals. If conservation is your main goal and MSC certification is only one element of the conservation plan, then the biological importance of an area may be your driving force for site selection. While this makes sense for conservation purposes, good fishery candidates that do not lie directly within an area of great biological importance can easily be overlooked. If developing sustainable fisheries is your main goal, then it might be beneficial to consider regions of varying biological importance. The importance of working in a biologically important location can then be considered down the line as one of your criteria for selecting a candidate fishery, and can be weighted appropriately. This approach will safeguard against limiting your options from the beginning.

4. Identify Experts

Now that you have done some planning and set the goals and geographical boundaries of your project, it is time to start looking for candidate fisheries. There may be hundreds of active fisheries in your selected region. In addition, these fisheries can likely be described by a broad range of characteristics, ranging from coastal to oceanic, artisanal to industrial, and community-based to government-managed. So, how do you narrow the field? How do you start to determine which fisheries meet the goals of your project?

Don't try to do it alone!

The first step is to identify a panel of experts that can help provide fisheries information and guidance. The panel should be composed of a wide variety of experts who can give you the most comprehensive picture of fisheries in your area: academics, research scientists, fishery managers, government officials, staff of non-profits or NGOs, fishers, and others that work in the fishing industry. You can add additional types of experts if you feel their knowledge and opinions will add to the process. The inclusion of a variety of experts can also help you spread the word about the MSC and your organization's goals in the region – a bit of additional outreach that will help the process run more smoothly later on.

Also consider the culture of the region you are working in when deciding on your approach to data-gathering and the types of experts to include in your panel. Working in some locations may require creative tactics to obtain the cooperation of various stakeholders.

5. Consult the Experts

Once you have identified your panel of experts, you are ready to start asking for advice. It is important that the experts are well informed about the MSC certification process and the goals of your institution. Informing participants is critical to obtaining the best information about fisheries in your region.

Pre-Analysis Workshop

Because the concept of ecolabeling and the MSC process are complex and often best explained in person, the most effective way to educate your panel is to bring them together at a workshop where you present your project goals and the MSC program. This provides a framework within which you can discuss appropriate candidate fisheries.

The Questionnaire

Once you have well-informed experts, we recommend asking them to complete a questionnaire as a way to gather information about potential candidate fisheries. You can have the experts fill out questionnaires as individuals or in groups at or after the workshop.

If a workshop is logistically or financially unfeasible, you can write a letter to the experts asking for their help. Be sure to provide the experts with a list of sources (e.g. websites) where they can learn more about the MSC and your organization. Also, remember that the experts might be very busy. If you do not receive a response from them, follow up by phone or e-mail, and perhaps ask for an appointment to interview them personally.

Three example questionnaires are provided. First is our general questionnaire. This questionnaire is very thorough, and we suggest that you adapt it for your region and specific goals. For example, although the majority of the questionnaire remained similar, the WWF Community Fisheries Program has for past projects modified several questions to reflect a data-driven culture in one region and data-limited conditions in another.

6. Review Recommendations

Once the experts have returned completed questionnaires, you should complete a fishery profile for each of the candidate fisheries. The profile is a way to compile all of the information that you have collected into a comprehensive overview of each fishery. Feel free to modify our example to capture the information you have gathered with your questionnaires. You will use the fishery profiles later on to evaluate your criteria in the software program.

It is extremely important that you enter accurate data into the software program, especially for quantitative criteria, as the information will affect your results. Therefore, we strongly recommend that you ground-truth the information you have collected. You might also need to seek out supplementary information that was not provided by the experts. You could do this by meeting with potential candidates to review the facts about their fishery and gage their interest in MSC certification. Since MSC certification is voluntary and requires the participation of the fishery, it is essential that your chosen candidate is interested in engaging in the process. You could also consult other stakeholders, published literature, or reliable online references such as FishBase.

After you have completed the fishery profiles and verified the information received, you should review the experts' recommendations and determine if the suggested fisheries fit with your institutional and project goals. Discuss the candidates with your project partners. At this point, you may want to remove one or more of the fisheries from consideration. However, if you would like to end with a database of all of the information you have gathered, we suggest narrowing the field of candidates at a later point in the process.

7. Design Your Database

Now that you have selected and gathered sufficient information about your candidate fisheries, it is time to design your database. The first step is to create a new project.

Create New Project

To create a new project, first click the File option in the top menubar and then click Create New Project. A new dialog box will open. Select a project type of Fisheries. Next, click the Browse button to choose a location and name for your saved project. Optionally, you may also load a set of suggested fisheries criteria for your analysis by clicking the Load Suggested Criteria checkbox. Suggested criteria are described in more detail below and can always be removed later. Once you are done providing all of the information for your new project, click OK to create it. Your project will load and you will see five tabs: Project Info, Alternatives, Criteria, Input Data, and Analysis.

Define Alternatives

Your alternatives are the various fisheries for which you have collected data. If you select the Alternatives tab, you will see an empty table. Click Add and Remove to enter and edit the list of fisheries you want to use in your analysis. When adding an alternative, a color will be chosen for you and associated with the alternative. You may choose a different color by clicking the "New Color" button. This will randomly pick a new color. Once you are finished, move on by selecting the Criteria tab.

Define Criteria

Criteria are specific standards that the software program will use to evaluate the candidate fisheries from biological, management, political, socioeconomic, and market perspectives. Criteria may reflect qualitative or quantitative information.

Over the course of a number of projects, we have developed a list of suggested criteria and guidance for their evaluation, which are built into the program. This list reflects lessons we learned while developing this process and covers the most important areas to consider when selecting a small-scale fishery to guide through the MSC certification process. You may load the suggested criteria into your project during the initial project creation phase, as described at the beginning of this section. Feel free to add to, delete, or modify the suggested criteria based on your specific project goals and location.

To add your own criteria, click the Add button to open the dialog. Next, enter a description of a new criterion. Then, identify the criterion as a benefit or a cost by clicking the appropriate radio button. Finally, select whether the criterion will be defined on a binary, ordinal, or ratio scale:

·        Binary criteria are qualitative criteria with only two options, such as for a yes or no question. If the criterion is a 'Benefit' then Option 1 is the ‘positive’ option and will score higher in analysis. Option 2 is the ‘negative’ option and will score lower in analysis. One example of a criterion that's a benefit is: "Community participation in fishery management". It makes sense in this case that Option 1 would be 'Yes' and Option 2 would be 'No' where 'Yes' is better than 'No'.

If the criterion is a 'Cost' then Option 1 is the 'negative' option and will score lower in analysis. Option 2 is the 'positive' option and will score higher in analysis.

Enter an appropriate description for each option (e.g. “yes” and “no,” “positive” and “negative,” “high” and “low”) and click OK.

·        Ordinal criteria are qualitative criteria with more than two options, which must be clearly defined. Add each option in sequence by clicking the Add Option button (you may also remove any option that has been entered by clicking the Remove Option button). For each option, enter a description (e.g. “Good,” “Moderate,” “Poor”) and assign it a rank. A rank relates this option to the other options for this criterion. Overall, an option with a higher rank value will score higher in analysis than an option with a lower rank value. For example, let’s say we have a criterion with five options ranking from 1-5. If this criterion has been labeled as a 'Benefit', then the option with a rank of 5 is seen as the most beneficial and the option with the rank of 1 is seen as the least beneficial. If this criterion has been labeled as a 'Cost', then the option with a rank of 1 is seen as the most costly and the option with a rank of 5 is seen as the least costly. Define all of your options and click OK.

·        Ratio criteria deal with quantitative information. If the criterion is labelled as a 'Benefit' then a larger value will score higher in analysis than a lower value. One example of a ratio criterion that is a benefit is "Catch volume" with unit "tons/yr." If the criterion is labelled as a 'Cost' then a lower value will score higher in analysis than a higher value. One example of a ratio criterion that is a cost is "Bycatch volume" with unit "tons/yr." Describe the units of the criterion (e.g. “tons/yr”, “people”, “days”) and click OK. 

Keep in mind that you are not required to use all of the criteria you enter here in your analysis – use this stage as a way to create a complete database of all of the information you have collected, regardless of whether it will factor into your evaluation.

In your full list of criteria, scroll to the right to see the type, options, and cost/benefit information for each criterion.

Input Data

When your list of criteria is complete, select the Input Data tab. A table appears with the fishery alternatives listed across the top row and criteria down the first column. Using your completed fishery profiles, you can either input data directly into the table or import the data into Delphos from an independent spreadsheet program.

Inputting Data Directly

Cells associated with qualitative criteria have drop-down menus containing the options that you previously defined. For each of these cells, select the appropriate option from the menu. Cells associated with quantitative criteria expect a single positive integer as input. Click inside each of these cells to enter a value.

Click the Save button at any time to save the input table. Any subsequent changes will not be saved unless you click Save. When you return to this project at a later time, your saved input will automatically load into the table. 

Importing Data

If you would like to prepare your database in a spreadsheet program such as Excel or OpenOffice Calc, you can export a template of the Delphos database in a comma-separated value (CSV) format. To export a template, click the Export button under the Input Data tab, enter a filename, and save the file to your hard drive. The template can then be opened in your spreadsheet program. The template will look similar to the data table in Delphos, with the fisheries listed across the top row and criteria down the first column. However, your options for evaluation will now appear in the second column. Instructions for entering data correctly are provided in the first column below the table - pay careful attention to them. An example template file with valid input values can be found in the data folder within the main Delphos folder that you downloaded.

Populate the template with input values based on the information you have gathered about each fishery, keeping in mind that all input values should be numeric. For qualitative criteria, the numeric values associated with each option are listed in the second column. Be sure to enter the number corresponding to the appropriate option for each cell, not the name of the option. Also, do not change the structure of the template, such as adding or removing columns or rows. If you make changes, the importing process is likely to fail or give you unexpected results.

Once you are done entering values, save the file in the CSV format. Next, return to Delphos and click the Import button. Select your file and click OK. Once the importing process is complete you will be notified whether it was successful or not. If the process was unsuccessful, an error message should appear that describes the problem. If the process was successful, the information from your template will appear in the data table. Double-check that the cells filled in correctly, and be sure to save the completed input table.

Data Gaps

You will likely find that you lack enough information to complete every cell in the table. This is fine for the purpose of creating a database, but the Evamix algorithm cannot evaluate fisheries with gaps in information. The criterion or fishery corresponding to each incomplete cell will later need to be dropped from the analysis.

To fill data gaps you will want to consult your panel of experts again. By relying on the experts for information, you help to ensure a robust evaluation. You may want to invite them to a workshop aimed at filling information gaps, where you divide them into small groups and ask the groups to assess each fishery against each criterion. This will facilitate discussion, creating a better flow of information and resulting in a more complete data set. If you are unable to have a workshop and must send the experts the evaluation table, you will need to average their opinions when the tables are returned. You could also again consult other stakeholders, published literature, or reliable online references such as FishBase.

Once the evaluation table is complete and you are satisfied with the robustness of the information, you are ready to analyze the data.

8. Run the Analysis

To begin the multicriteria analysis, select the Analysis tab. Here you can start new analysis runs and view any previously completed runs within the project. To start a new analysis, enter a name and description for the run (a brief description will later help you differentiate between analyses) and click Start Analysis. A new window will open with a list of your previously defined fisheries.

Select Alternatives

At this point you should have a good sense of which fisheries to include in the evaluation process. In Step 1 of the analysis, select the fisheries you want to evaluate by clicking the appropriate boxes in the first column of the table. We recommend selecting no less than 3 fisheries and keeping the upper limit to between 10-15 alternatives.

Click Next to move on to Step 2: Select Criteria.

Select Criteria

Select the criteria you want to evaluate by clicking the appropriate checkboxes in the first column of the table. You must use the same list of criteria for all fisheries under comparison. Also, remember that for every criterion and fishery you wish to include, all cells must be filled in or the analysis will not work. If you could not address some of the criteria, then those criteria or fisheries with data gaps must be dropped from the analysis.

Click Next to move on to Step 3: Input Data.

Input Data

In this step, you will see a table with the input data for the fisheries and criteria you have just selected. You may change the information in any of the cells at this step for the purpose of your analysis, but keep in mind that your changes will not be saved in your overall input database, only in the analysis run. As mentioned previously, because the Evamix algorithm cannot evaluate fisheries with gaps in information, all cells at this stage must be filled in or the corresponding fishery/criterion must be dropped from the analysis.

Click Next to move on to Step 4: Assign Weights. 

Weight Criteria

Weighting the criteria allows project partners to decide which criteria are most important and generate analysis results that reflect their different goals or perspectives. Criteria must be weighted on an ordinal scale where criteria with a lower weight are deemed more important than criteria with a higher weight. For example, if you were to use a weighting scale of 1-5, then criteria with a weight of 1 would be seen as most important and have the greatest impact on the final result. Criteria with a weight of 5 would have the least importance and the least impact on the final result. The numerical difference between weights has no impact on the results, however, as the program will convert your weights to an ordinal scale during the analysis.

Weight your criteria with any positive integer, and keep in mind that criteria weights have a large impact on the final result.

For example, say you are planning a vacation, and have narrowed your potential destinations to Mexico, Alaska, and Italy. Your choice will differ depending on the importance of various criteria such as weather, activities, and cost. If you dream of lying on a warm, sandy beach, you would prefer Mexico or Italy over Alaska. If cost is then important, and visiting Mexico would be less expensive than traveling to Italy, you might choose to go to Mexico. If cost is not as important to you as the availability of local wines, you might prefer Italy. On the other hand, if weather is not important to you and you want to see wildlife, you might pick Alaska. Your decision will vary based on how much weight you place on each of your criteria, just as in this program.

You may first want to have each partner in your project weight the criteria independently and run the analysis with each weighting scheme. The results will provide a basis for discussion. You can also run the model using an average of each partner's weights, as well as equal weights for all criteria.

Run the Program

At this point you are ready to run the model. Click Run Analysis. Once the analysis has completed, a summary of the results and inputs will appear and you will see the name of your analysis in the Previous Analyses box. The summary of each analysis can be accessed at any time by selecting a run and clicking Summary. You can also click Export to save the results of each summary as a CSV file, which can then be opened in Excel or a similar program.

You can run the model multiple times with differently weighted criteria, and thus evaluate the fisheries based on the different scenarios, goals, and viewpoints of your project partners. The ability to perform multiple analyses within a project also allows you to compare different groups of fisheries. For example, if you have fisheries from different countries in your dataset, you might want to compare fisheries in one country at a time. In this case, you might run the program a number of times using differently weighted criteria for each of your groups of fisheries.

To modify and re-run a prior analysis, click on the name of your analysis and then click Restart. You will be asked to enter a name and description for the new analysis (your original analysis will remain unchanged). Click Restart and you will be taken back to Step 1: Select Alternatives. Modify any of your previous selections as desired, and run the analysis as before. 

Evaluating Your Results

Each evaluation will produce a ranking of the fisheries analyzed, which is graphically displayed in each summary file. You will want to bring the project partners together to discuss any differences in rankings between analyses, what might be causing the differences, and which might be the best candidate(s) for MSC certification. Multiple summaries can be opened simultaneously, allowing you to easily compare the results of different analyses.

Be sure to focus your discussions around the rankings of the fisheries, not the score of the multicriteria analysis, which is not based on a ratio scale. In other words, the difference between scores is not proportional to the difference between fisheries. Also, keep in mind that the quality of your results is only as good as the quality of your data, and that the rankings are based on pairwise comparisons. Thus, your highest-ranked fishery is not necessarily certain to meet all of the MSC Principles and Criteria.

Next Steps

You have by this time built a support system of partners, colleagues, and stakeholders who helped you with this evaluation process. They would very much appreciate a report on your experiences as well as your final decision, with which you may want to include a note thanking them for their help.

You will also want to communicate your selection back to the stakeholders of the fishery you selected, as they will be excited to hear about the results of your analysis. Once you have confirmed the fishery's interest in MSC certification, contact the WWF Community Fisheries Program and/or the MSC for assistance with entering the pre-assessment process.

Conclusion

Delphos is a step-by-step process designed to help you select a good candidate fishery for MSC certification. As with any method, you should modify it to suit your needs. If you have any suggestions or feedback on either the pre-analysis method or the software program, please do not hesitate to contact us – your advice is more than welcome.
